B1. Important Scientific Achievements of the Project Leader (maximum 2 pages)
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Dr. Elena-Maria Ionescu is a senior researcher in biomedical signal processing
and applied machine learning, affiliated with the Department of Computer Science
and Artificial Intelligence at the Technical University of Cluj-Napoca. Over the
past decade, her research has been directed at the development of intelligent,
non-invasive diagnostic tools for neurological and neurodegenerative disorders,
with a consistent focus on acoustic and kinematic biomarkers. Her contributions
are organized below by research direction.

1. Development of Acoustic Feature Engineering for Clinical Screening

Dr. Ionescu pioneered the application of extended Mel-Frequency Cepstral
Coefficient (MFCC) analysis for pathological voice screening in Romanian-speaking
populations. Her methodological contribution involved the systematic validation
of acoustic indices – including jitter, shimmer, harmonics-to-noise ratio (HNR),
and formant dispersion – against clinical UPDRS-III motor subscale scores in a
cohort of 120 participants recruited in collaboration with the Neurology
Department of Cluj-Napoca County Hospital. This work established the first
Romanian-language acoustic reference dataset for dysarthric speech associated
with early-stage Parkinson's disease (PD), published in Computers in Biology and
Medicine (2021, IF = 7.0). The dataset has since been used in two subsequent
independent studies by research groups in Budapest and Warsaw, demonstrating its
scientific reproducibility and broader relevance.

2. Machine Learning Frameworks for Neurological Disease Classification

A second major research direction concerns the design of interpretable,
clinically translatable machine learning (ML) classifiers. Dr. Ionescu developed
a hybrid ensemble framework combining Support Vector Machines (SVM), Gradient
Boosting (XGBoost), and a shallow Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) branch for
feature extraction from raw spectrogram representations. The model, validated on
the publicly available PC-GITA corpus (50 PD patients, 50 healthy controls,
Colombian Spanish) and on her local Romanian dataset, achieved an area under the
ROC curve (AUC) of 0.921, outperforming individual model baselines by 6–11
percentage points. Crucially, the framework incorporates SHAP (SHapley Additive
exPlanations) attribution, enabling clinicians to trace individual predictions
back to specific acoustic features – a design choice motivated by regulatory and
ethical requirements for AI-based clinical decision support. Results were
published in IEEE Journal of Biomedical and Health Informatics (2022, IF = 7.7).

3. Gait Analysis Using Low-Cost Wearable Inertial Measurement Units

In collaboration with the West University of Timișoara and the Romanian Society
of Neurology, Dr. Ionescu led a feasibility study assessing stride-level gait
parameters extracted from a bilateral ankle-mounted IMU system in 45 PD patients
at Hoehn & Yahr stages I–III and 45 age-matched healthy controls. The study
demonstrated that a compact set of six spatiotemporal features (cadence, stride
length, stride time variability, stance duration, swing asymmetry index, and
double-support percentage) discriminated PD from healthy gait with 87.3%
sensitivity and 89.1% specificity using a Random Forest classifier. Critically,
data collection required only 10 minutes of corridor walking, confirming
feasibility for routine clinical deployment. This work was published in Sensors
(2023, IF = 3.9) and received the Best Paper Award at the IEEE International
Symposium on Medical Measurements and Applications (MeMeA 2023, Jeju Island).

C1. Motivation of the Proposed Theme in the Current Scientific Context.
    Originality and Degree of Innovation
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

1.1 Scientific and Socioeconomic Importance of the Problem

Parkinson's disease (PD) is the second most prevalent neurodegenerative disorder
worldwide, affecting more than 10 million individuals globally and an estimated
80,000–100,000 persons in Romania. The global prevalence has doubled over the
past three decades – from 2.5 million cases in 1990 to 6.1 million in 2016 – and
epidemiological projections indicate a further doubling by 2040 under current
demographic trends. This trajectory imposes a formidable and growing burden on
healthcare systems: in the United States alone, the annual economic cost of PD
exceeds 51.9 billion USD, equally split between direct medical expenditures and
indirect costs (loss of productivity, informal caregiving). Similar proportional
burdens are observed across European healthcare systems, including Romania, where
the progressive degradation of motor and cognitive functions in PD patients
translates into increasing demands on neurology services, rehabilitation
infrastructure, and social support networks.

The primary clinical challenge of PD lies not in its management once diagnosed,
but in the systematic and persistent failure to detect the disease at an early,
pre-symptomatic or minimally symptomatic stage. Current diagnostic practice is
entirely clinical: trained neurologists apply standardized motor examination
criteria (Movement Disorder Society-Unified Parkinson's Disease Rating Scale,
MDS-UPDRS) based on observable symptoms including resting tremor, bradykinesia,
muscular rigidity, and postural instability. No blood test, genetic screen, or
single imaging modality provides a definitive diagnosis. DaTscan (dopamine
transporter scintigraphy) can support differential diagnosis but cannot
distinguish PD from other parkinsonian syndromes and is not suitable for routine
early screening. As a consequence, diagnosis is typically established only when
60–80% of dopaminergic neurons in the substantia nigra have already undergone
irreversible degeneration – a point at which significant neurological damage is
permanent.

The clinical consequences of late diagnosis are severe. Epidemiological data
indicate that 17% of PD patients wait more than three years from the onset of
their first symptoms to confirmed diagnosis. Diagnostic instability is also
substantial: 13.3% of initial PD diagnoses are subsequently revised, most within
the first two years, reflecting the subjective and symptom-dependent nature of
current assessment methods. Each year of diagnostic delay represents a lost
opportunity to initiate neuroprotective therapies, optimise dopaminergic
management, and engage the patient in lifestyle modifications that have
demonstrated disease-modifying potential. Early diagnosis also delays the
initiation of levodopa therapy, extending the window of pharmacological efficacy
and reducing the cumulative levodopa dose associated with motor complications
(dyskinesia). From a health economics perspective, early detection is thus
strongly cost-effective over the long-term disease trajectory.

In Romania specifically, the challenge is compounded by geographic and structural
factors: neurological specialist services are concentrated in large urban centres
(Bucharest, Cluj-Napoca, Iași, Timișoara), while the majority of the elderly
population – the primary at-risk demographic – resides in rural or peri-urban
areas with limited specialist access. A non-invasive, low-cost, remotely
deployable screening tool would therefore address not only a scientific gap but
also a significant healthcare equity gap within the national context.

1.2 Limitations of Current Approaches and State of Knowledge

Two non-invasive biological signals have emerged in the past decade as
scientifically validated early-stage markers of PD progression: voice and gait.

Voice changes in PD – collectively termed hypokinetic dysarthria – manifest in
nearly 90% of patients and include measurable alterations in phonatory stability
(increased jitter and shimmer), reduced vocal loudness (hypophonia), decreased
pitch modulation range, prolonged pause duration, and imprecise articulation.
Crucially, these acoustic changes have been shown to precede visible motor
symptoms, with longitudinal analyses of celebrity speech recordings documenting
significant changes in pitch variability, pause duration, and speech rate up to
10 years before formal PD diagnosis (Rusz et al., npj Parkinson's Disease, 2024).
Neuroimaging studies have further established that the degree of vocal impairment
correlates with the putamen dopamine binding ratio, providing a direct mechanistic
link between nigrostriatal neurodegeneration and acoustic biomarkers.

Gait disturbances are among the primary motor manifestations of PD and are
mediated by dysfunction in complex cortico-basal ganglia-cerebellar motor
circuits. Characteristic gait abnormalities include shortened stride length,
reduced walking speed, increased stride time variability, festination, and
freezing of gait. Modern inertial measurement unit (IMU) technology enables the
precise, non-invasive quantification of these parameters during normal walking
tasks of 10–15 minutes, capturing spatiotemporal features that are sensitive to
early-stage disease (Hoehn & Yahr stages I–II).

Machine learning approaches applied to these signals have demonstrated promising
individual-modality classification performance: voice-based models achieve AUC
values of 0.85–0.93 on benchmark datasets; gait-based models achieve 0.81–0.95
depending on the feature representation and classifier architecture. However,
two critical limitations constrain the clinical translation of existing research:

(a) Single-modality limitation: the majority of published systems address voice
or gait in isolation. The few multimodal studies published to date (Faiem et al.,
2024; Zhao et al., 2022) report improved AUC values of 0.86–0.97, confirming
that data fusion significantly outperforms single-modality approaches, yet no
clinically validated, integrated multimodal platform exists in either research
or clinical practice.

(b) Language and population generalisability: nearly all available benchmark
datasets and validated models are based on Spanish-speaking (PC-GITA) or
English-speaking (mPower) cohorts. Romanian presents distinct phonological
characteristics (vowel inventory, consonant clusters, prosodic patterns) that
render direct model transfer unreliable. No Romanian-language PD dataset of
sufficient scale for deep learning training is currently available in the
scientific literature.

(c) Clinical interpretability gap: the majority of high-performing models are
deep neural networks that function as black boxes, preventing clinician trust,
regulatory approval, and integration into clinical decision support workflows.
Explainable AI (XAI) approaches remain a minority in the PD biomarker literature.

1.3 Elements of Originality and Innovation

NEURO-DETECT addresses all three identified limitations through four principal
innovations:

Innovation 1 – First validated Romanian multimodal PD biomarker dataset: The
project will collect and make openly available the first Romanian-language voice
corpus combined with synchronised IMU-based gait recordings from a prospective
clinical cohort, filling a critical gap in European PD biomarker research.

Innovation 2 – Multimodal late-fusion architecture with uncertainty
quantification: NEURO-DETECT will develop a novel late-fusion decision framework
that combines independently trained voice and gait classifiers via a learned
weighting mechanism informed by prediction confidence, enabling robust inference
even when one modality produces low-quality data (e.g., background noise, sensor
occlusion).

Innovation 3 – Longitudinal tracking capability: unlike cross-sectional systems,
NEURO-DETECT will be designed to track individual patient trajectories across
repeated measurements, providing a quantitative index of disease progression that
can inform treatment adjustment.

Innovation 4 – Clinician-facing explainability: all model outputs will be
accompanied by SHAP-based feature attribution maps presented in clinical language
(e.g., "elevated jitter and reduced stride length contributed most to this
screening result"), bridging the AI/clinical translation gap.

The proposed project has not been addressed in any prior project led by Dr.
Ionescu or by other Romanian research groups with equivalent scope. The closest
prior work (BrainSign, UEFISCDI 2022–2023) addressed single-modality acoustic
analysis in a smaller feasibility format; NEURO-DETECT represents a substantial
extension in scale, ambition, multimodality, and clinical validation depth.

C2. Objectives, Methodology and Work Plan
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

2.1 Concrete Objectives

The project will pursue four specific, measurable objectives:

O1 – Dataset Construction: Collect, annotate, and publicly release a bilingual
(Romanian/English instruction) multimodal PD dataset comprising voice recordings
and synchronised IMU gait data from a minimum of 100 PD patients (Hoehn & Yahr
stages I–III) and 100 age-matched healthy controls recruited through two Romanian
neurological clinical partners.

O2 – Unimodal Model Development: Develop, train, and cross-validate
state-of-the-art machine learning and deep learning classifiers for each modality
independently (voice module: target AUC ≥ 0.92; gait module: target AUC ≥ 0.90).

O3 – Multimodal Fusion System: Develop, train, and validate an uncertainty-aware
late-fusion architecture integrating the voice and gait modules, targeting a
multimodal AUC ≥ 0.95 and providing SHAP-based prediction explanations.

O4 – Clinical Validation and Usability Assessment: Conduct a prospective
clinical validation study of the complete NEURO-DETECT platform in a real
clinical setting, involving neurologists rating system usability (target: System
Usability Scale score ≥ 75) and assessing agreement between system screening
results and clinical MDS-UPDRS assessments.

2.2 Proposed Work Strategy and Methods

Work Package 1 (WP1) – Data Collection and Preprocessing

Voice data will be collected using a standardised protocol: participants will
produce three tasks – sustained phonation of the vowel /a/ for 5 seconds,
reading a standardised Romanian text passage (Aesop's "The Wind and the Sun",
adapted), and a 60-second semi-spontaneous narrative description. Recordings
will be made using a calibrated cardioid microphone (AKG C414, 44.1 kHz, 24-bit)
in a quiet clinical room. All participants will provide written informed consent
under a protocol approved by the Ethics Committee of Cluj-Napoca County Hospital.

Gait data will be collected using bilateral ankle-mounted IMU sensors (APDM
Opal, 128 Hz, 6-DoF) during a 10-minute walk along a 20-metre corridor at
self-selected speed, including U-turns. Synchronised kinematic data (3-axis
accelerometry, 3-axis gyroscopy) will be segmented into individual gait cycles
using a validated stride detection algorithm.

Clinical ground truth for all participants will include: MDS-UPDRS parts I–IV,
Hoehn & Yahr stage, Montreal Cognitive Assessment (MoCA), and medication status
(ON/OFF state for PD patients).

Work Package 2 (WP2) – Voice Analysis Module

Feature extraction will employ two parallel pathways: (a) handcrafted acoustic
features (jitter, shimmer, HNR, MFCC-1–13, formant frequencies F1–F3, pitch
statistics, spectral features) extracted using the openSMILE 3.0 and Praat
toolkits; (b) raw spectrogram representations input to a lightweight CNN. The
two feature spaces will be concatenated for joint classification. Models to be
evaluated: SVM with RBF kernel, XGBoost, Random Forest, LSTM networks, CNN-LSTM
hybrids. Model selection will be based on 5-fold stratified cross-validation;
final evaluation on a held-out 20% test set.

Work Package 3 (WP3) – Gait Analysis Module

Spatiotemporal gait features (stride length, cadence, stride time variability,
double-support time, swing time asymmetry index, step length asymmetry, and
freeze-of-gait index) will be extracted per-stride and aggregated into session-
level statistics (mean, standard deviation, coefficient of variation, 5th and
95th percentile). Additionally, raw accelerometry time series will be processed
through a 1D-CNN feature extractor for learning latent spatiotemporal
representations. Classification models will mirror WP2 for inter-modality
comparability.

Work Package 4 (WP4) – Multimodal Fusion

A late-fusion architecture will combine the probability output vectors of the
voice and gait models via a meta-learner (logistic regression and attention-
weighted averaging, compared). Uncertainty quantification will be implemented
using Monte Carlo dropout (Gal & Ghahramani approach), producing calibrated
confidence intervals for each prediction. The fusion model will be trained and
validated on the same 80/20 split, with modality drop-out augmentation to train
robustness to missing modalities. SHAP values will be computed for all features
contributing to the fusion output.

Work Package 5 (WP5) – Clinical Validation and Dissemination

The complete platform will be deployed as a lightweight desktop application (web
interface, Python/Flask backend) and evaluated in a prospective observational
study at two clinical sites. Neurologists will use the system as an adjunct to
standard assessment and rate its outputs via structured feedback (SUS, 5-point
Likert agreement scale). Agreements between system classification and clinical
diagnosis will be assessed using Cohen's kappa. Two open-access publications
will be submitted; the dataset will be deposited in Zenodo under CC-BY 4.0.

3.3 Preliminary Results Supporting the Working Hypothesis

The working hypothesis of NEURO-DETECT – that a multimodal, interpretable ML
system combining voice and gait features can achieve clinically relevant early
detection of PD in Romanian-speaking populations – is supported by the following
preliminary evidence generated by the research team:

(a) Romanian voice corpus (preliminary, n=60): A pilot voice dataset of 30 PD
patients and 30 healthy controls, collected during the BrainSign project
(2022–2023), demonstrated discriminative power of acoustic features with a
cross-validated AUC of 0.887 using an SVM-RBF classifier on MFCC and jitter
features. This confirms the transferability of acoustic biomarker methodologies
to Romanian phonological contexts.

(b) Gait feasibility study (published, Sensors 2023): As described in B1
(contribution 3), IMU-based gait classification achieved 87.3% sensitivity and
89.1% specificity in a 45+45 cohort using a compact 6-feature Random Forest
model, confirming protocol feasibility and expected classification performance
bounds.

(c) Multimodal pilot (unpublished): In a retrospective analysis of the 45
participants for whom both voice and gait data were collected during BrainSign
(opportunistic data), a simple score-level fusion (average of voice and gait
SVM probabilities) increased AUC from 0.887 (voice alone) and 0.861 (gait alone)
to 0.921 (fused), providing direct empirical support for the multimodal
hypothesis and justifying the methodological direction of WP4.

These results collectively demonstrate that the project team possesses the
technical capability, clinical access, and empirical evidence base necessary to
achieve the stated objectives.

C4. Risks and Alternative Approaches
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

The following risk assessment covers technical, operational, and ethical
dimensions of project implementation.

RISK 1 – Insufficient participant recruitment
Probability: Medium | Impact: High

Description: The target of 100 PD patients (Hoehn & Yahr I–III) and 100
controls may not be met within the recruitment window (M3–M12) due to patient
eligibility exclusions (severe dementia, uncontrolled tremor precluding IMU
attachment, refusal to participate), clinical staff availability, or competing
study protocols at partner sites.

Mitigation: A second clinical partner site (Neurology Department, Iuliu
Hațieganu University of Medicine and Pharmacy, Cluj-Napoca) has been identified
and has expressed preliminary willingness to participate; a formal collaboration
agreement will be signed during M1. If enrolment at the primary site is below
60% of target by M9, recruitment at the second site will be activated. The
minimum viable dataset for the machine learning objectives is set at n=80+80
(power analysis for AUC=0.92 at 80% power, α=0.05), below which the scope of
O3 (fusion) will be reduced to a proof-of-concept rather than a clinically
validated model.

RISK 2 – Technical failure or delay in IMU sensor procurement
Probability: Low | Impact: Medium

Description: Supply chain disruptions may delay delivery of the APDM Opal IMU
systems beyond the planned M2 delivery target, delaying data collection start.

Mitigation: An alternative sensor system (Mbientlab MetaMotionS, available
domestically through a local distributor) is compatible with the planned feature
extraction pipeline and has been validated in the PD gait literature. Procurement
can be completed within one week if the APDM order is delayed beyond six weeks.

RISK 3 – Model performance below target thresholds
Probability: Low-Medium | Impact: Medium

Description: The voice module (target AUC ≥ 0.92) or gait module (target AUC ≥
0.90) may underperform due to dataset heterogeneity (medication state variability,
age range, disease severity mix) or acoustic environment inconsistencies.

Mitigation: A pre-specified performance floor (AUC ≥ 0.85 for each modality) is
defined as a project-level minimum. If intermediate evaluation at M12 indicates
performance below this floor, two remediation strategies will be applied: (a)
re-examination and tightening of data quality control criteria (signal-to-noise
ratio thresholds for voice; gait cycle completeness criteria for IMU); (b)
integration of pre-trained speech representations (wav2vec 2.0 fine-tuning) as
an alternative to handcrafted feature extraction.

RISK 4 – Key personnel departure
Probability: Low | Impact: High

Description: The departure of the postdoctoral researcher during the project
would remove a critical technical resource, particularly for WP3 and WP4.

Mitigation: All code, models, and documentation will be maintained in a version-
controlled repository (GitHub, private during development, public at M20) with
weekly updated technical logs. The doctoral candidate (Role 3) is cross-trained
on gait analysis methods. If the postdoctoral position becomes vacant, the Project
Leader will assume technical supervision directly and activate the institutional
HR fast-track recruitment procedure (typical time-to-hire: 4–6 weeks for CS
postdoctoral roles at TUCN).

RISK 5 – Ethics approval delay
Probability: Low | Impact: Medium

Description: Ethics approval for clinical data collection from human subjects
involves variable timelines at both institutional (TUCN Ethics Committee) and
hospital (Cluj-Napoca County Hospital Ethics Committee) levels.

Mitigation: Ethics applications will be submitted in parallel during M1. The
data collection protocol (voice and gait only, no blood draw, no drug
intervention) represents a minimal-risk study category, historically associated
with approval timelines of 4–6 weeks at both institutions. If approval is delayed
beyond M3, retrospective analysis of the existing BrainSign pilot dataset will
commence as a substitute activity to maintain progress on WP2.

- Gait module: AUC ≥ 0.90 (target), ≥ 0.85 (minimum acceptable)
- Multimodal fusion: AUC ≥ 0.95 (target), ≥ 0.88 (minimum acceptable)
- Clinical usability: SUS score ≥ 75 (rated by ≥ 10 neurologists)
- Cohen's kappa (system vs. clinical diagnosis): ≥ 0.70 (substantial agreement)

5.2 Impact on the Host Institution and Research Team

NEURO-DETECT will reinforce TUCN as a nationally recognised centre for
AI-powered biomedical research. The project will produce two trained early-career
researchers (postdoctoral fellow, doctoral candidate) with expertise in clinical
AI systems – a profile in high demand across both academia and the growing
Romanian healthtech industry. The open-access dataset will serve as a teaching
resource in graduate-level ML courses at TUCN and can anchor future multi-centric
studies. The patent extension will strengthen the institution's intellectual
property portfolio and lay the foundation for a follow-on technology transfer
project with MedTech Romania S.R.L.

5.3 Broader Scientific, Technological and Societal Impact

Scientific: NEURO-DETECT will fill the Romanian-language gap in PD biomarker
research, enabling future cross-European comparisons and contributing to the
global effort to build language-diverse, population-representative PD datasets.
The multimodal uncertainty-aware fusion framework is a methodological
contribution applicable beyond PD to other neurodegenerative disorders (multiple
system atrophy, progressive supranuclear palsy, Alzheimer's disease).

Technological: The lightweight deployment architecture (Python/Flask web
interface, compatible with standard clinical workstation hardware) is designed
for low-barrier adoption by Romanian hospital IT infrastructure without requiring
cloud connectivity – a critical feature for hospitals in smaller cities with
limited internet bandwidth.

Societal: If translated to clinical practice, a system achieving AUC ≥ 0.95
would enable primary care physicians in rural areas to flag PD-risk patients for
specialist referral, potentially compressing the 1–3 year diagnostic delay by
6–12 months for a meaningful fraction of the Romanian PD population. The
consequent earlier initiation of therapy could delay nursing-home placement and
reduce informal caregiving burden at the household level.

5.4 Dissemination and Communication Strategy

Scientific community: Two open-access journal articles and two conference
presentations will ensure rapid dissemination within the biomedical AI and
neurology communities. Pre-prints will be posted to arXiv (cs.LG and eess.SP
sections) simultaneously with journal submission.

Clinical community: Results will be presented at the Annual Congress of the
Romanian Society of Neurology and at the European Parkinson's Disease Association
(EPDA) Scientific Congress (both planned for Y2). A clinical summary document
(2 pages, non-technical language) will be prepared and distributed through the
Romanian Society of Neurology mailing list.

General public: A press release co-authored with the TUCN Communications Office
will be issued at two project milestones (dataset publication, M20; final
results, M24). A brief explainer video (5 minutes, Romanian language) will be
produced and disseminated via the TUCN YouTube channel and the project website.

5.5 Open Publication Practices

All journal publications arising from NEURO-DETECT will be submitted to fully
open-access or hybrid open-access venues. Article Processing Charges (APCs) are
budgeted within the logistics expenditure line. Datasets, code, and model weights
will be released under permissive open licences (CC-BY 4.0 for data; MIT licence
for software) within six months of project conclusion or upon corresponding paper
acceptance, whichever is earlier.

5.6 Intellectual Property Rights

Background IP: The existing voice dataset (n=60, BrainSign project) and the
patent OSIM A/00362/2023 constitute background IP owned by TUCN. Their use
within NEURO-DETECT is governed by the existing institutional IP agreement.

Foreground IP: New IP generated by NEURO-DETECT (extended dataset, multimodal
fusion architecture, uncertainty quantification method) will be owned by TUCN as
host institution, in accordance with Romanian Law no. 64/1991 on patents. The
Project Leader and contributing team members will be listed as co-inventors.
Open-source code release (MIT licence) is compatible with institutional IP policy
and does not preclude patent protection for the implemented system, since the
patent covers the integrated diagnostic method rather than the individual
software components.
